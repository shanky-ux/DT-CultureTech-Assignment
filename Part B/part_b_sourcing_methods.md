# Part B: Question 1 — Sourcing Methods for Federer Companies
### DeepThought Business Analytics Internship

## How I Would Find Federer Companies Beyond Google Search

The challenge with Google search for ICP sourcing is that it surfaces whatever is optimized for the web — large companies with marketing budgets, listicles with the same 50 names repeated everywhere, and industry databases that show you what everyone else is already looking at.

The Federer company is typically not optimized for web visibility. It's a Rs.80Cr specialty chemical maker in Ankleshwar whose founder has a PhD from IISc and spends money on R&D, not SEO. You won't find it by searching "specialty chemical manufacturers India."

Here are the sourcing methods I would use — each with reasoning specific to this ICP.

---

## 1. DSIR Recognized In-House R&D Units List

**What it is:** The Department of Scientific and Industrial Research publishes a list of companies with formally recognized in-house R&D units. There are ~3,500 entries nationally, updated periodically.

**Why it works for this ICP:** DSIR recognition requires a formal application, a defined R&D facility, and qualified scientific personnel. This directly proves C3 (differentiation — they invest in R&D) and C4 (technical decision-maker — someone built and runs the R&D unit). It pre-qualifies two of six criteria before you even look at the company's website.

**How to use it:** Download from dsir.gov.in. Filter by state (Maharashtra, Gujarat, Karnataka, Telangana, Tamil Nadu) and industry category (pharmaceuticals, chemicals, biotechnology, electronics). Cross-reference with manufacturing NIC codes to remove service companies.

**Limitation:** Skews toward established companies that knew about DSIR and applied. Newer specialty manufacturers, especially in emerging segments (advanced agri-biotech, fluorochemicals), often haven't applied. Also updated with a lag — companies on the list may have changed significantly.

---

## 2. International Regulatory Approval Databases

**What it is:** USFDA publishes a searchable database of approved drug and device manufacturing facilities (fda.gov). EU-GMP database (European Medicines Agency) covers pharma manufacturing. WHO-GMP prequalification list covers facilities approved for global health procurement.

**Why it works:** Regulatory approval simultaneously proves C1 (physical manufacturing facility), C3 (differentiated product — commodity manufacturers don't seek USFDA approval), and C5 (export tailwind — they're already serving export markets). Three criteria proven in one step.

**How to use it:** USFDA database is searchable by country. Filter to India. Export results and remove Tier-1 large pharma (Sun, Cipla, Dr. Reddy's — too large). Focus on mid-tier companies with 1–3 approved facilities. Cross-reference with revenue data to estimate ICP band fit.

**Limitation:** Strong pharma and biotech bias. Doesn't cover defence, agri-inputs, specialty engineering, or industrial chemicals well. Also represents the compliance-forward subset of manufacturers — misses specialty manufacturers targeting domestic markets only.

---

## 3. Trade Expo Exhibitor Directories

**What it is:** Industry-specific trade expos publish exhibitor directories with company names, booth numbers, websites, and product descriptions. Key expos for this ICP:

- **BioAsia** (Hyderabad) — biotech, pharma, diagnostics, life sciences
- **CPHI India / PMEC India** — pharma ingredients, APIs, process equipment
- **India Chem / Chem Expo** — specialty chemicals, performance chemicals
- **Agri Intex** (Coimbatore) — agri-inputs, seeds, biopesticides, crop protection
- **India Lab Expo** — laboratory instruments, diagnostic tools, life science equipment
- **IMTEX** (Bengaluru) — precision engineering, machine tools, aerospace manufacturing
- **Aero India** — defence electronics, aerospace components

**Why it works:** Expo participation is a self-selection signal for C6 (growth mode). A company spending Rs.2–10L on an expo booth is signaling market development intent — not a wealth-preservation company. Expo segmentation also means you're already looking at the right verticals without additional filtering.

**How to use it:** Most expo websites publish PDF exhibitor directories. Last 3 years of directories (2022–2025) are the most useful — recent enough to reflect current company status. Scrape or manually extract exhibitor names and websites. Past expo archives available via Wayback Machine if current website doesn't retain old directories.

**Limitation:** Biased toward companies with marketing budgets and urban distribution. Misses bootstrapped manufacturers that grow entirely through long-term B2B relationships and don't market publicly.

---

## 4. State Industrial Development Corporation Directories

**What it is:** Each major manufacturing state maintains industrial estate directories:
- **GIDC** (Gujarat) — Ankleshwar, Vatva, Dahej, Panoli industrial estates
- **MIDC** (Maharashtra) — Pune, Tarapur, Nagpur, Ambernath
- **KIADB / KSSIDC** (Karnataka) — Peenya, Electronic City, Bommasandra
- **TSIIC** (Telangana) — Genome Valley, IDA Nacharam, Fab City
- **SIDCO** (Tamil Nadu) — Hosur, Coimbatore

**Why it works:** Industrial estate tenancy directly proves C1 (physical manufacturing facility in a specific industrial zone) and C2 (India-based, specific city). State directories often include product category descriptions and contact details. Less well-known than national databases, so lower competition from other researchers.

**How to use it:** GIDC and MIDC directories are available online. TSIIC's Genome Valley tenant list is publicly documented. For others, a direct inquiry to the SIDC office often yields a directory for nominal cost or free for stated research purposes.

**Limitation:** Data staleness is significant — companies may have moved, closed, or changed segment. These directories are typically updated annually at best. Always verify active status before using.

---

## 5. Government Scheme Beneficiary Lists

**What it is:** Several government schemes publish beneficiary lists — companies that have received grants, approvals, or recognitions:

- **PLI scheme beneficiaries** (Ministry of Commerce — pharma, chemicals, medical devices, food processing, electronics)
- **DPIIT Startup India recognized companies** (filtered to manufacturing verticals)
- **Make in India certified companies** (specific manufacturing categories)
- **Startup India funded companies** in biotech/medtech (BIRAC grants, SIDBI SAFE fund)
- **Export Import Bank of India funded SMEs** in target sectors

**Why it works:** PLI scheme beneficiaries are definitionally growth-mode companies — they've committed to investment and production targets to qualify for incentives. DPIIT Startup recognition in manufacturing signals a technical founder (required for recognition). This directly signals C5 (tailwind — government support received) and C6 (growth mode — committed capital deployment).

**How to use it:** PLI beneficiary lists are published by the respective ministry websites (pharma PLI: Department of Pharmaceuticals; specialty chemicals: Ministry of Chemicals). BIRAC grant recipients are published on birac.nic.in.

**Limitation:** PLI lists are public only at announcement stage — updates may not be publicly available. DPIIT recognition database is large and noisy (many tech startups, not manufacturers). Requires heavy filtering.

---

## 6. Patent and Publication Databases

**What it is:** Search Indian Patent Office (ipindia.gov.in) and Google Patents for manufacturing-related patents filed by Indian MSMEs. Also search Google Scholar and PubMed for research papers with affiliation from Indian companies (not universities — company R&D).

**Why it works:** This is the purest signal for C3 (differentiation — they have IP) and C4 (technical decision-maker — the inventor is often the founder). A company with 3–5 product patents filed in the last 5 years is a textbook Federer candidate.

**Specific search:** IPO patent search filtered to Indian corporate applicants in chemistry, biotech, and electronics classes. Filter out universities, large corporations, and MNC Indian subsidiaries.

**How to use it:** The Indian Patent Office search portal allows classification-based search. Google Patents is more powerful for full-text search. Filter by applicant type (company, not individual or university), applicant country (India), and filing year (2018–2024 for recent activity).

**Limitation:** Many specialty manufacturers do NOT patent their processes — trade secrets are preferred in chemistry where patent disclosure creates reverse-engineering risk. Patent absence is not a disqualifier. Also, patent search is time-intensive at scale.

---

## 7. Alumni Networks of Science and Technical Institutions

**What it is:** IIT (all campuses), IISc, BITS Pilani, UICT Mumbai, ICT Mumbai, and central government research labs (CSIR, DRDO, ISRO, BARC) alumni networks have entrepreneur members who have founded specialty manufacturing companies.

**Why it works:** This is the highest-reliability source for C4 (technical decision-maker). IIT/IISc alumni founder = nearly guaranteed to be the scientist-founder archetype. The alumni networks often maintain directories of entrepreneur members.

**How to use it:** IIT alumni portals (alumni.iitb.ac.in, etc.) have searchable member directories. LinkedIn search of IIT/IISc alumni who are "Founder" or "MD" at manufacturing companies. LinkedIn alumni filter for these institutions filtered by "currently at company with 50–500 employees in manufacturing."

**Limitation:** Alumni network directories are often incomplete and require institutional access. LinkedIn approach is better but requires Sales Navigator and significant manual time. Also biases toward founders who maintain an active professional online presence — which some successful MSME founders deliberately don't.

---

## 8. Sector-Specific Journalists and Industry Reports

**What it is:** Business journalists covering specialty chemicals, pharma, biotech, and manufacturing verticals often write profiles of companies outside the mainstream. Publications like Chemical Weekly, Pharmabiz, BioSpectrum India, and Express Pharma regularly feature mid-sized manufacturers.

**Why it works:** A company featured in Chemical Weekly or BioSpectrum India has already been identified as notable within its sector by a domain journalist — a human-filtered signal that is harder to fake than SEO-optimized content. These publications also often mention specific financial details and founder backgrounds that company websites don't.

**How to use it:** Search Chemical Weekly archives (available online) for company names in specific segments. BioSpectrum India, Pharmabiz, and Agropages India have searchable article archives. Build a list from featured company names over 2022–2025.

**Limitation:** Coverage biases toward press-release-friendly companies. Contrarian, head-down manufacturers who don't pitch to journalists won't appear. Also requires paid access for some archive databases.

---

## 9. Reverse Geography — Industrial Belt Mapping

**What it is:** Use Google Maps to identify industrial estates and manufacturing clusters in target cities, then systematically research companies visible within those clusters.

**How:** Search "pharma industrial estate Ankleshwar" or "biotech park Hyderabad" on Google Maps. Look at satellite view to identify factory/plant buildings. Cross-reference with GIDC/MIDC tenancy data to identify company names at those locations. This identifies manufacturers who are completely invisible to web search.

**Why it works:** The most bootstrapped, web-invisible Federer companies have a physical plant that appears on Google Maps even when they have no digital presence. This is the only sourcing method that can find these companies.

**Limitation:** Labor-intensive at scale. Useful as a supplementary method for specific industrial clusters where other sources have low yield. Not automatable without significant engineering (Google Maps API + satellite image analysis).

---

## 10. Cold Triangulation via Supply Chain Analysis

**What it is:** Large public companies (Cipla, Sun Pharma, Nestle, Hindustan Unilever, Asian Paints) publish annual reports that mention key suppliers. These suppliers — who supply specialty inputs to large companies — are often exact Federer profile companies.

**How:** Read annual reports or CSR disclosures of large Indian manufacturers in target sectors. Identify mentioned suppliers, contract manufacturers, or R&D partners. Research each mentioned company for ICP fit.

**Why it works:** A small specialty manufacturer supplying to Cipla or Sun Pharma is almost definitionally differentiated (large companies wouldn't source a commodity from a small vendor — they'd go direct to commodity suppliers). This is a reliable C3 signal.

**Limitation:** Most annual reports don't disclose supplier names for competitive reasons. This method works best with companies that have supply chain transparency initiatives or detailed CSR reporting. Also works for companies that have received public recognition as supplier partners.

## Summary Table

| Method | Primary Criteria Signaled | Scalability | Speed | Uniqueness |
|--------|--------------------------|-------------|-------|------------|
| DSIR R&D list | C3, C4 | High | Fast | Medium |
| USFDA / EU-GMP databases | C1, C3, C5 | High | Fast | Medium |
| Expo exhibitor directories | C6, segment match | High | Medium | Medium |
| State industrial estate directories | C1, C2 | Medium | Medium | High |
| PLI / govt scheme beneficiaries | C5, C6 | Medium | Medium | High |
| Patent / publication databases | C3, C4 | Low-Medium | Slow | High |
| IIT/IISc alumni networks | C4 | Low | Slow | High |
| Industry press archives | C3, C6 | Medium | Medium | High |
| Google Maps industrial belt mapping | C1 | Low | Very slow | Very high |
| Supply chain triangulation | C3, C5 | Low | Slow | Very high |

**For the 1000-company proposal (Part B, Question 2):** Sources 1–4 are high-volume and automatable — they build the bulk of the universe quickly. Sources 5–8 are medium-volume and add unique companies that the high-volume sources miss. Sources 9–10 are low-volume but high-precision — used to find specific high-value targets and fill gaps.
